What’s crazy about that series is how much he knows about graphics cards and physics, and how he uses approximations to optimize his code. His solutions are often genius but also surprisingly simple. It’s very entertaining and informative about computer science and graphics programming, and it's one of the few accessible entry points into this field.

Here, he tries to simulate the ocean. The thing is, waves must be both random and realistic. What I love about his video is how technical he gets with shader code, like OpenGL shaders, and how patient he is. He runs into very complex, high-level issues but still manages to come back to them, sometimes even months later.

He made an insanely good series where he explains how he developed a sound engine in C++ (the engine works for any mechanical simulation). What I love about him is not just his videos even though they’re really good but the freedom he chooses to keep. For example, he could have easily sold that sound engine, but he didn’t. He made it open source so everyone can use it. That shows how committed he is to sharing knowledge and resisting the temptation of easy profit. He also keeps updating it based on user feedback and needs, even though it’s completely free.
